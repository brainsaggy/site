My Story

They Called It Muscle Spasms. It Was a Hole in My Spine.

On New Year's Eve 2012, I walked into a major academic medical center with headaches and back pain I couldn't explain. The pain was in my thoracic spine. The headaches were aching, band-like. Something was wrong. That was the beginning of a 13-year medical odyssey.

By 2015, I was in ERs multiple times — tearful, depressed, dealing with what they called "migraines." I described pain at the base of my skull like being hit with a hammer, pain in my actual eyeballs, back pain that radiated up to my head. The thoracic x-ray showed "kyphotic straightening suggesting muscle spasm." That's as far as they looked.

"I spent 13 years being dismissed, gaslit, and told I was fine — while diverticula at T12-L1 were actively leaking cerebrospinal fluid."

Over the next decade, I tried physical therapy, acupuncture, muscle relaxants, gabapentin, nerve blocks — nothing worked because they were treating symptoms, not the cause. An MRI was denied in 2018. Doctors kept writing "muscular" in my chart. I couldn't sit for more than 15 minutes. I couldn't work. I lost relationships because the pain and fog changed who I was as a person.

In August 2025, I experienced the worst flare-up of my life. I became desperate. I started pulling my own medical records, reading radiology reports, studying anatomy. On September 14th, I spotted a dark area on my own 2015 x-ray — in the exact location where I'd always felt pain. I raised it with my PCP. He spent most of that visit explaining why it probably wasn't what I thought it was.

When I reported clear fluid leaking from my nose onto my bedsheets while sleeping, I showed a neurologist the stain. He told me that if it were CSF, I'd "probably be dead." So I guess I'm a zombie. I'm still here. Still leaking. Still fighting.

On November 18, 2025, a neurosurgeon finally looked at my MRI and saw what no one else had been willing to see: dilated nerve root sleeves at T12, a large diverticulum expanding the left L1 foramen, and a 13-year history consistent with spontaneous intracranial hypotension. My brain was literally sagging inside my head. That's not a metaphor — it's a documented medical phenomenon called "brain sag."

I thought that was the end of the fight. It wasn't. A CT myelogram came back "unremarkable" — even though the imaging clearly shows bone erosion at the leak site, visible on the slides. The same neurosurgeon who validated my 13-year nightmare essentially dropped me because I wasn't actively leaking during the test. Diagnosed and abandoned in the span of a few months.

This isn't a story about being smarter than doctors. It's a story about what happens when the system fails you — over and over — and you have no choice but to keep fighting. I lost meaningful relationships. I lost years of my life to a fog that no one could explain. I'm still trying to get this fixed. I'm sharing this because too many people are living in the same fog, being told the same things, and they deserve to know that getting a diagnosis doesn't mean getting treated.

Understanding the Condition

What Is a Spinal CSF Leak?

Cerebrospinal fluid cushions your brain and spinal cord. When it leaks out through a tear, hole, or diverticulum in the spinal dura, the resulting low pressure can cause debilitating symptoms that are frequently misdiagnosed.

Intracranial Hypotension

When cerebrospinal fluid leaks from the spinal canal, the volume and pressure inside the skull drops. The brain, no longer fully supported by its fluid cushion, sags downward — a phenomenon visible on MRI. This is why the condition is sometimes called "brain sag." The resulting symptoms can mimic everything from migraines to dementia, which is why it's so frequently misdiagnosed.

Symptoms

  • Positional headaches (worse upright, better lying down)
  • Neck pain and stiffness
  • Cognitive fog / "brain fog"
  • Tinnitus or muffled hearing
  • Visual disturbances
  • Nausea and dizziness
  • Pain between the shoulder blades
  • CSF rhinorrhea (clear fluid draining from the nose)

What Gets Missed

  • Told it's "just migraines"
  • Attributed to anxiety or stress
  • Labeled as muscle spasms
  • Diagnosed as fibromyalgia
  • MRI requests denied as "unnecessary"
  • CSF rhinorrhea mistaken for allergies or sinus issues

Types of CSF Leaks

  • Dural tear — a rip in the membrane surrounding the spinal cord
  • Bone spur puncture — calcified disc material piercing the dura
  • Meningeal diverticulum — an outpouching where CSF pools and may leak
  • CSF-venous fistula — an abnormal connection draining CSF into a vein

Getting Diagnosed

  • Brain MRI with contrast — may show pachymeningeal enhancement, brain sagging, or subdural collections
  • CT myelogram — the gold standard for locating the actual leak site
  • Digital subtraction myelography — for detecting CSF-venous fistulas
  • Spinal MRI — may show epidural fluid collections or the diverticulum itself

The Journey

13 Years of Being Dismissed

This is what the road to a self-diagnosis actually looks like. Over a decade of appointments, dismissals, one fleeting validation, and a fight that still isn't over.

December 2012
The First Visit
New Year's Eve. First PCP visit at a major academic medical center for headaches and thoracic back pain. Pain described as aching, band-like. Aggravated by position. Associated with back pain. Noted as a "new problem."
2015
ER Visits and Dismissed Imaging
Multiple ER visits for what they called "migraines." I described pain where my skull meets my spine "like being hit with a hammer." Thoracic x-ray showed kyphotic straightening — the radiologist wrote "muscle spasm." Pain in my actual eyeballs. Tearful. Depressed. Sent home every time.
2016–2022
The Lost Years
Physical therapy. Acupuncture. Muscle relaxants. Gabapentin. Nothing worked. In 2018, my doctor requested an MRI — insurance denied it. Point tenderness at L1 was noted over and over, always attributed to "musculoskeletal" causes. I couldn't sit for more than 15 minutes. Couldn't work. Lost relationships because the pain and fog changed who I was.
August 2025
The Breaking Point
The worst flare-up of my life. Three days of head pain radiating from the back of my skull. I became desperate. Started pulling my medical records. Reading every radiology report. Learning anatomy. My PCP diagnosed occipital neuralgia. I knew it was something more.
September 2025
"I Am Desperate"
I told my PCP I believed I had a cerebrospinal fluid leak. He spent most of the visit explaining why he didn't think so. I reported clear fluid draining from my nose. On September 14th, I found a dark area on my own 2015 x-ray — in the exact location of my pain spot. My PCP's reply: "If it is not mentioned in the radiology report it is unlikely pathological."
October 2025
"You'd Probably Be Dead"
A neurologist evaluated me. Documented 12 years of severe headaches. Noted my T12 pain spot. I showed him a photo of a massive CSF leak stain on my bedsheet from fluid leaking out of my nose while sleeping. He told me if it were CSF, I'd "probably be dead." Considered a leak "unlikely." Suggested a headache clinic.
November 18, 2025
Confirmed
A neurosurgeon finally saw what no one else would. MRI showed dilated nerve root sleeves at T12 and a large diverticulum expanding the left L1 foramen. Assessment: 13-year history consistent with spontaneous intracranial hypotension. Suspected intermittent CSF leak from nerve root diverticula. Thirteen years. Validated in one appointment.
Early 2026
"Unremarkable"
CT myelogram ordered to locate the leak. The report came back "unremarkable" — despite imaging slides clearly showing bone erosion at the site, worn away over 13 years of leaking. The same neurosurgeon who diagnosed me essentially dropped my case because I wasn't actively leaking during the test. Diagnosed and abandoned.
2026 — Present
Still Fighting
Waiting on answers. Searching for a provider who will actually treat this. Pursuing accountability for the misdiagnosis. Building resources for patients stuck in the same cycle — because I've learned that even getting diagnosed doesn't mean the fight is over. You shouldn't have to diagnose yourself. But if you do, you sure as hell shouldn't have to beg for treatment.
For Those Who Care About Us

When Someone You Love Has a CSF Leak

This condition doesn't just affect the person who has it. It changes everything — personalities, relationships, plans, futures. The person you knew starts canceling plans, sleeping all day, struggling to think. They seem like a different person. They are.

Low CSF pressure affects the brain directly. It can alter mood, cognition, energy, and behavior. If someone you love has been diagnosed — or is fighting for a diagnosis — the most important thing you can do is believe them. Even when doctors don't.
