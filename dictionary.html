Know What They're Saying

Medical-to-Layman Dictionary

Radiology reports and medical records are written in a language designed for doctors, not patients. Here's a translation guide built from real reports and real experience — so you can actually understand what they're telling you (and what they're not).

Unremarkable
The radiologist didn't see anything they think matters. It does not mean nothing is there. It means nothing caught their eye — and that's exactly the word that kept my leaking spine hidden for 13 years.
Imaging & Reports
Impression
The radiologist's bottom-line summary. This is what your doctor reads first (and sometimes only). If something is wrong but isn't mentioned here, it may never be followed up on.
Imaging & Reports
Findings
The detailed section of a radiology report describing what was actually seen on the images — size, shape, location, density of anything notable. Read this section carefully. The Impression may leave things out.
Imaging & Reports
Clinical Indication [KLIN-ih-kul in-dih-KAY-shun]
The reason your doctor ordered the imaging, written at the top of the report. Example: "chronic mid-back pain — check structure." This tells the radiologist what to look for. If it's vague, the radiologist may not look hard enough.
Imaging & Reports
Comparison: None
The radiologist had no prior images to compare against. This means they can't tell if something is new, growing, or changing. If you've had prior imaging, this might mean they didn't pull your old studies.
Imaging & Reports
Archimedes' Principle [ar-kih-MEE-deez PRIN-sih-pul]
Any object (like your brain) submerged in a fluid (CSF) is buoyed up by a force equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces. When CSF volume drops, you lose that buoyancy — the brain literally feels heavier and begins to sag downward.
Imaging & Reports
Incidental Finding [in-sih-DEN-tul]
Something the radiologist noticed that wasn't related to the reason you were scanned. Could be meaningless — or could be the thing that matters most. Don't ignore it just because they call it "incidental."
Imaging & Reports
Technique / Views
How many images were taken and from what angles (AP, lateral, oblique). If the report says "2 views" but 4 were actually taken, that's a red flag — it may mean not all images were reviewed.
Imaging & Reports
AP View / Lateral View
AP (anteroposterior) = front-to-back shot. Lateral = side view. Different views reveal different things. A lesion visible on a lateral may be invisible on an AP, which is why reviewing all views matters.
Imaging & Reports
Preserved
"Disc space heights are preserved" = the spaces between your vertebrae look normal in height. Radiologists say this to indicate that discs haven't collapsed or degenerated. It doesn't mean everything is fine.
Imaging & Reports
Focal [FOH-kul]
Concentrated in one specific spot, not spread out. "Focal abnormality at T12-L1" means something is happening at one precise location. This word should make you pay attention — especially if it matches where you feel pain.
Imaging & Reports
Contrast / Gadolinium [GAD-oh-LIN-ee-um]
A dye injected into your bloodstream (for MRI) or spinal canal (for myelogram) to make certain structures light up on imaging. "Enhancement" on MRI with contrast can reveal inflammation or leaks that are invisible without it.
Imaging & Reports
Sagittal / Axial / Coronal [SAJ-ih-tul / AX-ee-ul / kor-OH-nul]
The three planes an MRI slices through your body. Sagittal = side view (splits you left/right). Axial = top-down cross-sections. Coronal = front view (splits you front/back). Different lesions are best seen on different planes.
Imaging & Reports
T1 / T2 Weighted (MRI)
Two ways to process MRI signals. On T1, fluid appears dark; on T2, fluid appears bright (white). CSF leaks and diverticula are often best seen on T2 images because the fluid lights up. If your doctor only ordered T1, they may miss a fluid-filled structure.
Imaging & Reports
Lesion [LEE-zhun]
Any area of abnormal tissue — could be a mass, a cyst, a tear, a scar, or almost anything that looks different from normal. It's a deliberately vague word. Ask what kind of lesion it is.
Imaging & Reports
Soft Tissue Shadows
On x-rays, overlapping muscle, fat, or organs can create shadows that obscure bone detail. Radiologists sometimes use this as a reason they couldn't see something clearly. It can be legitimate — or it can be a way to dismiss a finding they didn't investigate.
Imaging & Reports
Thoracolumbar Junction (T12-L1) [thor-AK-oh-LUM-bar]
Where the thoracic spine (mid-back) meets the lumbar spine (lower back) — around the bottom of your ribcage. This is a vulnerable transition zone and a common location for CSF leak sources like nerve root diverticula.
Anatomy
Paraspinal / Paraspinous [pair-uh-SPY-nul / pair-uh-SPY-nus]
The muscles running alongside your spine. "Left paraspinal tenderness at L1" means there's a painful spot in the muscles right next to your spine on the left side. If the same spot is tender for years, it may not be the muscles — it may be what's underneath them.
Anatomy
Dura / Dural [DUR-uh / DUR-ul]
The tough outermost membrane surrounding your brain and spinal cord. It holds cerebrospinal fluid in. When it tears or has a weak spot, CSF can leak out — that's the root cause of spontaneous intracranial hypotension.
Anatomy
Nerve Root Sleeve / Nerve Root Diverticulum [dy-ver-TIK-yoo-lum]
A small pouch or outpouching where the dura balloons out around a nerve root as it exits the spine. They can be normal variants, but when they enlarge, they become weak points where CSF can leak. These are what were found at my T12 and L1.
Anatomy
Foramen (Neural Foramen) [for-AY-men (NUR-ul for-AY-men)]
The bony opening on each side of a vertebra where a nerve root exits the spinal canal. "Expanding the left L1 foramen" means something (like a diverticulum) has grown large enough to widen the hole — which shouldn't be happening.
Anatomy
Neuroforaminal Encroachment [nur-oh-for-AM-ih-nul en-KROCH-ment]
Something (bone spur, disc bulge) is narrowing the opening where a nerve exits the spine, potentially pinching the nerve. Can cause pain, tingling, or weakness in the area that nerve supplies.
Anatomy
Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) [seh-REE-broh-SPY-nul]
The clear, colorless fluid that surrounds and cushions your brain and spinal cord. Your body produces about 500ml per day and reabsorbs it in a continuous cycle. When it leaks out, gravity pulls your brain downward — that's brain sag.
Anatomy
Foramen Magnum [for-AY-men MAG-num]
The large opening at the base of your skull where the brainstem connects to the spinal cord. When CSF pressure drops, the cerebellum can herniate (push down) through this opening, compressing vital structures.
Anatomy
Cerebellum [sair-uh-BELL-um]
The structure at the base of your brain — controls balance, coordination, cognition, and emotion (far more than most people realize). It's the part most directly affected by brain sag, because it sits right above the foramen magnum.
Anatomy
Occipital Region [ok-SIP-ih-tul]
The back of your head, where the skull curves down toward the neck. This is where many CSF leak headaches are centered — a relentless pain at the base of the skull that radiates upward over the scalp and often down to the eyes.
Anatomy
Epidural Space [ep-ih-DUR-ul]
The area between the dura and the bony walls of the spinal canal. When CSF leaks out of the dura, it often collects here first. An "epidural fluid collection" on MRI can be a sign of an active or recent leak.
Anatomy
Thecal Sac [THEE-kul sak]
The fluid-filled tube of dura that surrounds and protects your spinal cord and nerve roots. Think of it as the waterproof bag holding your spinal cord and CSF in place. When a report says "effacement of the thecal sac" or "compression of the thecal sac," it means something is pushing against that bag — a disc bulge, a bone spur, or swelling. If there's a tear in it, that's where CSF leaks out.
Anatomy
Spontaneous Intracranial Hypotension (SIH) [spon-TAY-nee-us in-truh-KRAY-nee-ul hy-poh-TEN-shun]
Low pressure inside the skull caused by a spinal CSF leak that occurred without trauma or medical procedure. "Spontaneous" means it happened on its own. The brain sags, the headaches are often positional (worse standing, better lying flat), and it's widely underdiagnosed.
Diagnoses
Kyphotic Straightening [ky-FOT-ik]
Your thoracic spine (mid-back) has lost its natural forward curve and appears too straight. Often attributed to muscle spasm. In my case, this was the entire conclusion — "suggests muscle spasm" — while a diverticulum sat right there on the image, unreported.
Diagnoses
Pachymeningeal Enhancement [pak-ee-men-IN-jee-ul]
The thick outer lining of the brain (pachymeninges) lights up on contrast MRI. This is one of the hallmark signs of intracranial hypotension. It means the lining is inflamed and thickened — often because the brain is sagging and pulling on its coverings.
Diagnoses
Occipital Neuralgia [ok-SIP-ih-tul nur-AL-juh]
Diagnosis for sharp, shooting pain at the back of the head along the occipital nerves. It's a real condition — but it's also commonly used as a convenient label when doctors can't explain recurring severe headaches. In my case, it was treating a symptom of the leak, not the cause.
Diagnoses
CSF Rhinorrhea [ry-nor-EE-uh]
Clear cerebrospinal fluid leaking from your nose. Most people don't know this can even happen. It can look like a runny nose or allergies, but the fluid is often watery, clear, and drips from only one nostril. A beta-2 transferrin test can confirm it's CSF.
Diagnoses
Cervical Spondylosis [SER-vih-kul spon-dih-LOH-sis]
Age-related wear and tear on the discs and bones of the neck (cervical spine). It's extremely common and often "found" on imaging. Doctors may point to it as the cause of your neck pain — even when the real problem is something else entirely.
Diagnoses
Tonsillar Herniation / Ectopia [TON-sih-lar her-nee-AY-shun / ek-TOH-pee-uh]
The cerebellar tonsils (bottom tips of the cerebellum) have been pushed downward through the foramen magnum. Can look like Chiari I malformation on imaging. In SIH patients, this isn't a birth defect — it's caused by the brain sagging from low CSF pressure.
Diagnoses
Unclear Etiology [ee-tee-OL-oh-jee]
"We don't know what's causing this." You'll see this in your chart when doctors can't explain your symptoms. It should trigger further investigation — but often it becomes a dead end. Ten years of my records said "unclear etiology."
Diagnoses
Musculoskeletal [mus-kyoo-loh-SKEL-ih-tul]
Related to muscles and bones. When doctors say your pain is "musculoskeletal in nature," they mean they think it's coming from muscles or joints — not nerves, organs, or structural problems. It's the default label when nothing obvious shows up on imaging.
Diagnoses
SEEPS (Mnemonic)
What doctors look for on brain MRI to diagnose SIH: Subdural fluid collections, Enhancement of pachymeninges, Engorgement of venous sinuses, Pituitary enlargement, Sagging of the brain. If your MRI shows any of these, push for a CSF leak workup.
Diagnoses
Brainstem Slumping / Sagging
The actual radiology term for what this site is named after. When CSF volume drops, the brain physically sags downward, compressing the brainstem against the skull base. On MRI, the brainstem appears flattened and displaced. This is considered specific for intracranial hypotension — if a radiologist sees it, there should be no question about what's happening.
Diagnoses
Subdural Fluid Collections [sub-DUR-ul]
Fluid that accumulates between the brain's surface and the dura. When the brain sags, it pulls away from the inner skull, and the gap can fill with fluid or even blood (subdural hematoma). This is one of the SEEPS signs and can be a serious complication of prolonged intracranial hypotension.
Diagnoses
Venous Engorgement
The large veins (venous sinuses) inside the skull appear swollen and distended on MRI. When CSF volume drops, blood volume increases to compensate — the Monro-Kellie doctrine. Convexity of the transverse sinus is one of the most common imaging findings in SIH (seen in ~75% of cases).
Diagnoses
Pituitary Enlargement [pih-TOO-ih-tair-ee]
The pituitary gland at the base of the brain appears swollen on MRI. Part of the SEEPS findings. When CSF pressure drops, blood volume in the skull increases to compensate, and the pituitary — which is highly vascular — engorges. It can mimic a pituitary tumor if the radiologist isn't thinking about SIH.
Diagnoses
CSF-Venous Fistula (CVF) [FIS-tyoo-luh]
An abnormal connection between the spinal fluid space and a vein, allowing CSF to drain directly into the bloodstream. This is a relatively recently recognized cause of SIH and can be extremely difficult to detect — standard CT myelograms often miss them entirely. Digital subtraction myelography (DSM) is usually needed to find them.
Diagnoses
Monro-Kellie Doctrine [mun-ROH KEL-ee]
The principle that the skull is a fixed container: brain, blood, and CSF must always add up to the same total volume. If CSF drops, blood volume increases to fill the gap. That's why SIH causes venous engorgement, pachymeningeal enhancement, and pituitary swelling — the body is compensating for the missing fluid.
Anatomy
Mamillopontine Distance [mam-IL-oh-PON-teen]
The distance between the mammillary bodies and the top of the pons, measured on a sagittal MRI. Normal is about 7mm. Below 5.5mm is a quantitative sign of brain sag. This measurement turns a subjective "does the brain look low?" into an objective number — and it's one of the most useful tools for diagnosing intracranial hypotension when the other signs are subtle.
Imaging & Reports
Pontomesencephalic Angle [PON-toh-mez-en-seh-FAL-ik]
The angle between the midbrain and the pons on sagittal MRI. Normal is around 65°. Below 50° is a sign of brain sag — the brainstem is being compressed and flattened. Like the mamillopontine distance, this gives radiologists a hard number instead of a judgment call.
Imaging & Reports
Suprasellar / Prepontine Cistern Effacement [SOO-pruh-SEL-ar / pree-PON-teen SIS-tern eh-FAYS-ment]
The CSF-filled spaces around the base of the brain appear compressed or absent on MRI. Normally these cisterns (fluid pockets) are clearly visible. When the brain sags, it squashes these spaces flat — "effacement" means they've been obliterated. A suprasellar cistern of 4mm or less is a major diagnostic sign.
Imaging & Reports
ABCs (X-Ray Review Mnemonic)
Alignment, Bones, Cartilage, Soft tissue. This is the systematic checklist radiologists are trained to follow when reading an x-ray. Every structure, every angle. In practice, time pressure and volume can turn this into a glance. If your report seems thin, ask: did they actually go through the ABCs?
Imaging & Reports
ABCDEF (Chest X-Ray Review)
Airways, Bones, Cardiac silhouette, Diaphragm, Edges (pleura/costophrenic angles), Fields (lung). A systematic approach to ensure nothing gets missed on a chest x-ray. When a radiologist writes a two-sentence report, ask yourself how thoroughly they walked through this list.
Imaging & Reports
Satisfaction of Search (SOS)
A documented cognitive error where finding one abnormality causes the radiologist to stop looking for others. Once they spot something, the brain says "found it" and moves on. Studies show detection of additional findings drops significantly after the first one is identified. If your report mentions one finding, there may be more they didn't look for.
Imaging & Reports
The 30% Problem
Studies spanning 75 years have consistently shown that radiologists miss significant findings on approximately 30% of abnormal studies. The real-time error rate averages 3–5% — applied to over a billion imaging studies worldwide, that's roughly 40 million errors per year. This isn't about bad doctors. It's about a system that asks humans to maintain perfection under impossible volume.
Imaging & Reports
Decision Fatigue
As radiologists read more studies in a shift, their accuracy drops. The brain takes shortcuts under mental strain, especially later in the day or during high-volume shifts. Your imaging study read at 4pm on a Friday after 200 other cases may not get the same attention as the one read at 9am. It's not malice — it's human cognition under industrial-scale demand.
Imaging & Reports
Premature Closure
When a doctor lands on a diagnosis early and stops considering alternatives. "Kyphotic straightening, suggests muscle spasm" — case closed, move on. But what if the straightening was caused by a diverticulum pressing on structures? Premature closure is one of the most common cognitive biases in medicine, and it thrives when there's pressure to read faster.
Imaging & Reports
“Check Structure” (Clinical Indication)
When your doctor orders imaging with a vague clinical indication like "check structure" or "chronic back pain," it tells the radiologist almost nothing about what to look for. A vague order gets a vague read. Compare that to "evaluate T12-L1 for structural abnormality, history of positional headaches and CSF rhinorrhea." The more specific the order, the harder they look.
Imaging & Reports
MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)
Uses magnets and radio waves (no radiation) to create detailed images of soft tissues. Best for seeing the brain, spinal cord, discs, and fluid collections. Critical for evaluating CSF leaks. An MRI "with contrast" adds gadolinium dye to highlight inflammation.
Procedures & Tests
CT Myelogram [MY-el-oh-gram]
Contrast dye is injected into your spinal canal via lumbar puncture, then a CT scan is taken. Considered the gold standard for pinpointing where a CSF leak is. But here's the catch: if you're not actively leaking during the test, it can come back "unremarkable" — even if you absolutely have a leak.
Procedures & Tests
Digital Subtraction Myelography (DSM) [my-el-OG-ruh-fee]
A more advanced version of myelography that uses real-time fluoroscopy to watch contrast dye flow through the spinal canal. Especially useful for detecting CSF-venous fistulas, which regular CT myelograms often miss.
Procedures & Tests
Epidural Blood Patch [ep-ih-DUR-ul]
A treatment where your own blood is injected into the epidural space to seal a CSF leak. It's often the first treatment tried. Works well for many patients, but may need to be repeated or targeted to the specific leak site to be effective.
Procedures & Tests
Beta-2 Transferrin Test [BAY-tuh-2 trans-FAIR-in]
A lab test that analyzes fluid (from your nose or ear) to determine whether it contains CSF. Beta-2 transferrin is a protein found almost exclusively in cerebrospinal fluid. If the test is positive, it confirms a CSF leak.
Procedures & Tests
Lumbar Puncture (Spinal Tap) [LUM-bar PUNK-chur]
A needle is inserted into the lower spine to measure CSF pressure or collect fluid for testing. Low opening pressure can support an SIH diagnosis — but normal pressure doesn't rule it out. Some SIH patients have normal pressure readings.
Procedures & Tests
Occipital Nerve Block
An injection of numbing medication around the occipital nerves at the base of the skull. Can temporarily relieve headache pain — but if the real problem is a CSF leak, the relief is masking a symptom, not treating the cause.
Procedures & Tests
Positional / Orthostatic Headache [or-thoh-STAT-ik]
A headache that gets dramatically worse when you stand up and improves when you lie down. This is the classic hallmark of a CSF leak. If you have this pattern, say the words "positional headache" to your doctor — it should trigger a specific workup.
Symptoms
Photophobia [foh-toh-FOH-bee-uh]
Sensitivity to light. During a flare, even normal room lighting can feel unbearable. It's a common feature of both migraines and CSF leak headaches — which is partly why they get confused for each other.
Symptoms
Phonophobia [foh-noh-FOH-bee-uh]
Sensitivity to sound. Everyday noises can feel amplified and painful. Another symptom shared between migraine and SIH that can make diagnosis harder.
Symptoms
Tinnitus [TIN-ih-tus]
Ringing, buzzing, or whooshing sounds in the ears with no external source. In SIH, it can be caused by changes in pressure affecting the inner ear structures. It's one of those symptoms that gets brushed off or attributed to stress.
Symptoms
Diplopia [dih-PLOH-pee-uh]
Double vision. Brain sag can stretch the cranial nerves that control eye movement, causing you to see two of everything. This is a serious neurological symptom that should prompt immediate investigation.
Symptoms
Cognitive Fog / Dysexecutive Symptoms [dis-eg-ZEK-yoo-tiv]
Difficulty thinking, concentrating, remembering, or making decisions. CSF leak patients often describe feeling like their brain is "wrapped in cotton." Cerebellar compression from brain sag can directly cause these cognitive issues — it's not anxiety, it's not depression, it's your brain being physically displaced.
Symptoms
Exacerbated by Valsalva [eg-ZAS-er-bay-ted by val-SAL-vuh]
Pain gets worse with straining — coughing, sneezing, bearing down during bowel movements, or heavy lifting. These actions increase spinal pressure, which can push more CSF through a leak or put strain on a diverticulum. If this describes your headache triggers, tell your doctor.
Symptoms

This isn't every medical term that exists — it's the ones that matter when you're reading your own records and trying to figure out what's actually going on. It also includes terms about how the system works (and doesn't) — because understanding why things get missed is just as important as understanding what was missed.
