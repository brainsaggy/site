Physiology & Key Principles

Term Phonetic Full Definition
Monro-Kellie Doctrine [MON-ro KEL-ee DOK-trin] The skull is a rigid, fixed-volume box. The three things inside it (brain tissue, blood, and cerebrospinal fluid) must always add up to the same total volume. If CSF leaks out, the blood vessels have to swell to “make up the difference” — this is the body’s emergency compensation.
Archimedes’ Principle [ar-kih-MEE-deez PRIN-sih-pul] Any object (like your brain) submerged in a fluid (CSF) is buoyed up by a force equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces. When CSF volume drops, you lose that buoyancy — the brain literally feels heavier and begins to sag downward.
Buoyancy Compensation [BOY-an-see kom-pen-SAY-shun] The brain’s “float” inside the CSF. When the fluid level drops, the brain loses its normal buoyancy and starts to sink or pull on pain-sensitive structures (dura, nerves, veins).
Venous Hypervolemia [VEE-nus hy-per-voh-LEE-mee-uh] The Monro-Kellie compensation where blood vessels (especially veins) swell inside the skull to replace lost CSF volume. In severe leaks this can extend outside the skull, causing scalp swelling or tenderness.
Dynamic Monro-Kellie Compensation [dy-NAM-ik MON-ro KEL-ee kom-pen-SAY-shun] The real-time, fluctuating version of the Monro-Kellie doctrine. Symptoms can come and go quickly because the body is constantly adjusting blood volume to try to keep intracranial pressure stable.
